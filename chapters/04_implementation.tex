\chapter{Implementation}
This chapter provides an overview of the work done to develop the MiWo platform, covering the robot's physical design and construction, expressive motion design, and locomotion via reinforcement learning.
The main goal of this thesis was to construct a robot with a high level of articulation and the capability to communicate emotion and intent through combined movements. Achieving natural and expressive motion across a platform with 16 degrees of freedom requires careful design of both hardware and software. The final platform assumes a biped form factor because it offers inherent familiarity to human observers, which is particularly important when evaluating how well the robot communicates emotion and intent. Legged systems also offer a high potential for adaptability and stability in different environments and terrains.

The implementation can be divided into 2 parallel work-streams: the physical system and the software systems used to generate motion. The physical/hardware side includes mechanical design, 3D printing, electronics, and actuators. The software side covers the ROS2 control architecture, inverse-kinematics solvers, an expressive motion framework, and the reinforcement-learning pipeline for generating locomotion. 

This chapter begins with the physical design and construction of the robot, followed by the software architecture that ties the system together. Expressive motion design is then presented, covering the kinematics and strategies for generating expressive motion. Finally, the reinforcement learning used to derive the gait will be discussed.

\section{Design and construction of the robot platform}
\subsection{Leg architecture and design}
The robot stands on 2 legs, each built with 5 motors, forming a 5-degree-of-freedom kinematic chain. The leg assembly was designed to efficiently control the position and orientation of the robot's legs relative to the body. Starting from the ground up, the chain begins at the ankle joint, controlling the foot's forward-and-back pitch. Moving upward, we have the knee joint, which controls the forward-and-backward pitch of the lower leg. The upper leg then connects to the hip assembly. 

\textbf{The hip assembly} consists of 3 consecutive joints: hip pitch, hip roll, and hip yaw. One design choice to simplify the calculations of inverse and forward kinematics was to model the hip as a spherical joint. By arranging the 3 rotational axes of the hip joints to intersect at a common point, we can simplify the kinematics by decoupling the legs' position and orientation.

\textbf{The feet} were designed with modularity in mind to facilitate testing on different surfaces. Each foot consists of 2 separate parts: An upper component connected directly to the ankle joint and a removable sole that slides smoothly into the upper section and is secured by a small, angled 3D-printed peg. The sole section of the foot has a rounded bottom, allowing the robot to shift its weight more easily across the rounded exterior during lateral movement. This design allowed for easy replacement or testing of different sole geometries. Examples include modifying the degree of rounding under the sole or testing different textures or materials to improve grip/stability without needing to reprint the full foot assembly.

The shin-and-femur link was designed to be intentionally asymmetric, with a more organic curvature rather than straight rods. This was done to make the robots' appearance more lifelike, supporting the platform's expressive goals. The idea was that a more natural, animal-like design would enhance the robot's appearance and make it feel more alive to human observers.

\subsection{Body Frame and Torso}
The two leg assemblies are connected by a central base link that serves as the pelvis and the platform's structural foundation. 
\textbf{The Base link} is directly connected to the left and right hip yaw motors and serves as the reference frame for kinematic computations. As this is the central link of the robot, it was designed to be strong and rigid enough to withstand the forces generated by the robot's legs.  The base link was also designed to safely test kinematics by incorporating a circular hole to accommodate a carbon fiber rod that provides support during initial inverse kinematics testing. In addition to the base frame, a small bracket was designed to house a circular linear bearing, allowing smooth, supported vertical motion. To support the carbon fiber rod, a small stand was also designed to keep it straight and allow it to be clamped to a table for increased stability. This was a more novel approach to testing the kinematics, as it was not the ideal material for building a safe and secure test rig; however, all the materials mentioned above were easily available at the time.

\textbf{The internal frame} follows the base frame and can be split into two parts: a lightweight outer frame and a stringer inner frame. The inner frame was designed to provide a larger structural foundation for mounting the outer frame and the neck pitch motor housed in the torso. The outer frame is mounted on the inner frame structure and serves as scaffolding to support the outer shell and house the robot's internal electronics and cable management.

\textbf{The internal components} of the torso are organized as follows: The battery rests on top of the base link to maintain a stable center of gravity and is held in place by a thin clamp secured directly to the base link. The clamp secures the rectangular battery on 3 sides, allowing the battery to slide out for easy removal. Above the battery is a small basket attached to the outer frame, designed to house the internal electronics, including the IMU and the DC/DC converter. The backside of the robot's torso provides a larger area of free space to house the excess length of wires connecting the various electrical components.

\textbf{The outer shell} of the robot was designed to give it its final appearance and was mounted directly to the torso's outer frame. The outer shell was designed to be modular and is separated into 5 different parts. The front and back plates were split into upper and lower parts, allowing quick reprinting of damaged sections without having to reprint the entire shell. The final part of the outer shell is the section that is mounted directly above the base frame. This part was designed to be easily removed, providing the user with easy access to the battery and internal electronics and allowing service and battery replacement without removing the entire outer shell.

\subsection{Head and Neck Architecture}
The head and neck assembly provides 6 degrees of freedom, allowing a wide range of expressive motions, including nodding, tilting, turning, and secondary actions involving antenna movements. The kinematic chain of the upper body begins with the neck pitch motor, which is mounted directly to the inner frame of the robot's torso.
 
The neck link is responsible for controlling the head's position and was designed to mount directly to the neck pitch motor and to house the head pitch motor, starting the chain that controls the position and orientation of the robot's head. At this point in the kinematic chain, the larger robotic actuators are replaced with smaller Dynamixel(XM430) servomotors due to their smaller form factor and the lower torque requirements of the upper body.

\textbf{The head links} connect the head pitch, yaw, and roll motors that control the orientation of the robot's head. Unlike the hip assembly, the tree head rotation axes do not form a spherical joint. This was a deliberate decision, as the head would be controlled by directly commanding each joint angle, making a full kinematic chain unnecessary and thereby simplifying both the mechanical design and the control software.
Designing these links was one of the more challenging aspects of the head assembly. Official Dynamixel side-mounting screws are short, limiting the thickness of the links to approximately 1.5 mm, matching the official metal brackets. Replicating this with PLA proved challenging, as these were the only links that consistently failed during testing. This issue was solved by adding recessed indents around screw holes to improve thread engagement, reinforcing high-stress areas with supporting structures, and increasing the wall loop count in the slices before printing the parts.

\textbf{The face link} forms the structural base and was designed to be both rigid and lightweight to reduce backlash and strain on the roll, pitch, and yaw motors controlling the head. The face link is mounted directly to the head roll motor and serves as the housing for a significant amount of electrical components. These include: the NVIDIA Orin serving as the onboard computer, two 1.46-inch LCDs for the eyes, two 9g servos for antenna actuation, an Arduino nano for servo control, a U2D2 board for communicating with the Dynamixel servos, an Adafruit servo driver board, a voltage regulator, and multiple USB cables allowing for serial communication across all components through the NVIDIA Orin nano. The face link was also designed to mount components neatly. Each component would have a dedicated mounting location with either raised screw bosses extruding from the bed of the face link or recessed grooves that allow components to be seated in place before being fastened. Some components required dedicated mounts, such as the two LCD screens and the two 9g servos. Additionally, a series of spare mounting holes was included in the base of the face link to allow for future components to be added without requiring a redesign of the full face link. To complete the head design, a simple, lightweight, easily removable top cover was added to provide access to the robot's internal components.

It should be noted that the face link was originally designed around a different set of internal components. When the initial components did not work as intended, they were replaced, but the face link design was retained because it still provided sufficient, compatible mounting for the new components. By designing the face link with spare mounting capabilities, it could accommodate hardware changes without requiring a full redesign.

\subsection{Materials and Fabrication}
All structural components and links of the robot platform were fabricated using PLA filament with a Bambu Lab A1 or A1 mini 3D printer. PLA was chosen for its affordability, availability, and lightweight nature, making it well-suited for a platform where weight and accessibility were key priorities in the design process. By fabricating all parts using 3D printing, the platform's cost was minimized, and different iterations could be tested physically within hours of being modeled, supporting the rapid prototyping approach used for this project. The use of widely available materials and commercial 3D printers ensures that the platform can be easily replicated by other researchers or developers, thereby contributing to the research field by making it open-source.

\textbf{The assembly} of the final platform uses 3 categories of fasteners, depending on the component being attached. All links mounted directly to the GO-M8010-r actuators use M3.5 screws for the stator and M4 screws for the Rotor. The links mounted on the Dynamixel XM430 servos use the official mounting screws supplied with the motors. The remainder of the assembly was mounted using standard M3 screws in either 10mm or 18.25mm lengths, depending on the required engagement depth.

\section{Robot Model}
\subsection{URDF Description}
The physical robot was designed in Autodesk Fusion and exported as a URDF (Unified Robot Description Format) model for use within the ROS2 system. The URDF format is an XML-based file format used in ROS to define the physical structure of the robot, including its links, joints, inertial properties, visual and collision geometry, and its kinematics and dynamics. The translation from Fusion to URDF was done by using the URDF2Fusion GitHub repository \cite{toshinori2020fusion2urdf}. This repository provides a fusion script that allows the user to export the project directly as a URDF. The script exports the robot description by traversing the assembly's component hierarchy, generating the corresponding URDF links and joints, along with STL mesh files for the visual and collision geometry of each link. In addition to generating the URDF file, the script will create a small ROS description package, including launch and YAML files, to simulate the robot in both Gazebo and RViz. This provided a ready-to-use starting point for building the simulation environments.

Before running the script, the fusion had to be prepared to meet a set of requirements, a process that required multiple attempts to achieve stable results. All distinct robot links were defined as separate components in the assembly, and the base component was renamed \texttt{base\_link} as required by the script. All controllable joints were defined as revolute joints, and time was taken to correctly assign parent and child components, as incorrect assignment would cause the exporter to fail.

The final exported URDF file served as a solid foundation for the robot description, but some manual changes were required before it could be used in the simulation. The inertial properties of each link, including mass and inertia tensors, were estimated from the fusion model, using the physical material properties automatically provided by the fusion script. The script had difficulty estimating the inertia of smaller, irregularly shaped links, resulting in inaccurate or non-positive-definite exported values and instability in the Gazebo simulation. This was solved by manually calculating estimates for the problematic links and replacing the exported values with the new estimates in the URDF file.

The mock hardware interface allows the full ROS2 control stack to run in simulation mode. This allows all control nodes, bridge nodes, and animation nodes to be tested against the visual model without risking damage to the actual hardware.

\section{ROS2 System Overview}
The software system for the Miwo platform was developed using the ROS2 Humble framework and runs on a device with Ubuntu 22.04. ROS 2 was used because it provides a modular architecture with individual nodes that communicate by subscribing to and publishing data on named channels (topics) or by calling services and actions. This architecture allows the final system to be broken down into smaller, independently runnable components, facilitating a more modular development and testing process.

The software system can be divided into 3 different hierarchical levels, as illustrated in Figure ~\ref{fig:software_architecture}. The lowest level, responsible for low-level system control, is called the hardware interface layer. The hardware interface layer consists of a set of bridge nodes. The bridge nodes are responsible for translating the ROS2 commands into low-level motor commands and reading back the hardware state, allowing us to control the hardware directly. One level above sits the state estimator layer, which processes raw IMU sensor data to compute the robot's current orientation and body pose. The uppermost layer is the motion control layer, which generates body pose commands based on the provided input. The input may come from joystick commands, expressive motion sequencer nodes, or a reinforcement learning policy. The layer is then responsible for translating these commands into joint trajectories for each motor using an inverse-kinematics solver.

\subsection{Hardware Interface Layer}
The hardware interface layer, as mentioned, is responsible for direct communication between the ROS2 system and the robot's physical actuators. This layer consists of 5 bridge nodes, each responsible for bridging the ROS2 system and the control protocol of the different actuators.

\subsubsection*{Unitree Motor Bridges}
The Unitree Motor bridges handle the communication between the ROS2 system and the 11 GO-M8010-6 actuators that control the two legs and the neck. There are 3 separate bridges: the left leg, the right leg, and the neck bridge, each communicating with its respective motors, split across 2 serial ports—one covering the left leg and the other covering the right leg and the neck.

Separating the motors across different serial ports was motivated by 2 main reasons. First, each serial port has a fixed bandwidth, and having all eleven motors on a single connection created a communication bottleneck, where motors had to wait for each other before their commands could be sent. This introduced latency and jitter into the control loop, causing undesired motion in the system. Second, running each bridge across 2 ports with its own control loop would ensure that communication errors on one port or bridge do not block or affect the operation of the other port or bridge. This made it easier to isolate the sources of errors and increased the system's overall fault tolerance.
Each bridge node operates with an internal control loop at 50 Hz, receiving joint trajectory commands from the ROS2 controllers and sending low-level position, velocity, and torque commands via the Unitree Actuator SDK. The bridge node interpolates the incoming trajectory waypoints using a first-order hold scheme to ensure smooth motion. The interpolation is also paired with a low-pass filter with a cutoff frequency of 37.5 Hz applied to the motor, similar to the work of \cite{grandia_design_2024}. Joint limits are also enforced in software to prevent commands that could damage the hardware. The Bridge also employs a gradual gain ramp-up period at startup to prevent sudden, large torques when the motors are first enabled.
The bridges were designed to start when the robot is placed on its dedicated calibration stand, which holds it in a fixed neutral position with the legs fully extended and the neck pitched back. On startup, each bridge will read the current encoder position of all motors and store it as the zero reference point for that run. The following joint angle commands are then interpreted relative to these zero positions instead of using absolute encoder values. This ensures that the robot starts from a consistent and known configuration for each run. This approach was implemented to account for potential encoder drift accumulation or power-cycle inconsistencies between runs, which could cause the robot to move to incorrect positions and damage the hardware. Upon startup, there is also a programmed smooth forward pitch to ensure all Unitree actuators reach the zero position specified in the robot's URDF file. 

\subsubsection*{Dynamixel Head Bridge}
The Dynamixel head bridge handles communication between the 3 XM430 servo motors that control head roll, pitch, and yaw. The Dynamixel motors build a smaller kinematic chain and communicate over a common TTL bus using the Dynamixel SDK and the Dynamixel communication Protocol. A U2D2 USB communication converter handles the communication between the onboard computer and the servos. 
The bridge node operates similarly to the Unitree bridge, providing 2 modes based on incoming commands. For continuous real-time control, such as joystick input, the bridge subscribes to the joint trajectory commands published on the head controller topic and then translates them into Dynamixel position commands. For smooth motion commands, such as moving to a predefined pose, the bridge uses a FollowJointTrajectory action server that accepts a goal trajectory and interpolates smoothly between the start and goal positions over a specified period.
The node also applies a deadband threshold to incoming position commands to prevent unnecessary motor activity from small or noisy commands. Additional configurations were also applied to the motors before deployment, directly on the servo hardware using the official setup wizard. The motors were configured with a smooth motion profile by setting the profile velocity and acceleration to low values, resulting in a pleasant, smooth motion. Motor limits were also enforced directly in the motor setup.
The logic of the Dynamixel bridge differs from that of the Unitree bridge at startup, as it reads the current positions of all three servos and stores them as the initial tracked positions. The 3 servos have predefined idle position values corresponding to the head at zero degrees of roll, pitch, and yaw. This approach provides a known, previously calibrated neutral head position to which we can command the robot to return when not actively controlling it. If a servo fails for some reason and prevents torque from being enabled on startup, the bridge will automatically attempt to reboot the affected servo before retrying, further increasing the reliability of the startup sequence.

\subsubsection*{Antenna control bridge}
The antenna bridge controls the two 9g servos that drive the robot's antenna movement. Unlike the other two bridges that communicate directly with the onboard computer over a serial connection, the antenna servos are driven by an Adafruit PCA9685 16-channel PWM servo driver board. The PCA9685 is a dedicated PWM controller that generates the PWM signals required to position the servo motors. The PCA9685 communicates with an Arduino Nano over the I2C bus; the Arduino receives the position commands from the onboard Jetson over a USB serial connection. This chain of communication (Jetson - Arduino - PCA9685 - Servo) was chosen because the Jetson does not natively generate hardware PWM signals suitable for the servos, and the Arduino provides a simple, widely available solution to bridge the communication gap. The bridge node subscribes to joint trajectory messages published to the antenna controller topic and translates the commands into PWM signals forwarded to the Arduino Nano. The Arduino runs a dedicated script that forwards the PWM signals received to the PCA9685, updating the servo positions. The antennas are not involved in the robot's balance or inverse kinematics and act independently, allowing movement to be layered on top of other motions. In the context of expressive motion, the antenna's motion will serve as a secondary action, as previously mentioned in the animation principles.

\subsubsection*{Eye Control Node}
The onboard computer controls the robot's eyes via an independent node. The node communicates with the two LCDs over Wi-Fi, sending expression commands corresponding to a predefined set of expressions, such as [happy, sad, squint, angry, idle]. The node also supplies the robot with realistic blinking by periodically publishing a blink command at random intervals with a randomized set of different blink animations, including [fast blink, single eye blink, slow blink, asymmetrical blink], which have different weights to give a more lifelike blinking experience. In addition to sending blink signals, the node subscribes to an eye command topic, allowing the animation nodes discussed later to publish eye commands at specific points in longer expressive motions.

\subsection{State Estimation}
\subsubsection*{IMU pipeline}
The IMU pipeline processes raw sensor data from the Phidgets spatial IMU to produce a stable estimate of the robot's orientation. The raw data from the IMU is published to the Phidgets ROS2 driver and then fed into a Madgwick filter that fuses the 2 sensor streams to produce a quaternion orientation estimate. The Madgwick filter was chosen for its computational efficiency and accurate orientation tracking, which match the system's requirements. The resulting orientation is then continuously published as a standard ROS2 message to a separate state node for further processing.

\subsubsection*{Robot State Node}
The robot state node serves as the central state monitor for the robot. The node subscribes to the filtered IMU messages and the robot's Cartesian body state. This node also serves as a simple GUI for the robot's user, displaying control modes, available actions, the published roll, pitch, and yaw in degrees, and the robot's Cartesian state in x, y, and z. Upon startup, the node will also perform an automatic IMU orientation calibration by collecting IMU data for 2 seconds while the robot sits in the calibration stand, setting the current orientation to [0,0,0]. A manual recalibration service is also provided, allowing the user to reset the IMU offsets at any point during testing.

\subsection{Motion control}
\subsubsection*{Cartesian Body Controller}
The Cartesian body controller serves as the central control node for the robot's motion. The controller subscribes to body pose commands expressed as a 6-DOF Cartesian pose with position [x, y, z] in millimeters and orientation [roll, pitch, yaw] in degrees, and translates them into joint-angle targets for the 10 leg actuators using an inverse-kinematics solver. The resulting joint trajectories were published to the right- and left-leg controller topics, where the hardware bridge nodes relay the commands to the physical hardware. The robot's current internal Cartesian state is also continuously published to a dedicated topic, allowing other nodes to read the current body position and use it as a basis for generating relative motion offsets.
\subsubsection*{Leveling Node}
The leveling node was implemented to provide automatic body leveling based on feedback from the available IMU data. It works by subscribing to the IMU orientation estimates and then computing corrective body twist commands to counteract any roll or pitch errors relative to the calibrated reference level. The correction is computed using a quadratic damping scheme that applies large corrections when the error is large and slows the correction as the error is minimized, preventing overshoots and oscillations. The leveling can be activated at any time via a ROS2 service call, allowing selective use during operation. The leveling node was introduced to address an issue observed after the robot was removed from the calibration stand. Specifically, the robot’s legs exhibited slight compliance, causing a small but noticeable change in orientation when placed on solid ground without external support. Under the influence of gravity, the robot's posture showed a minor sag. By implementing the leveling node, the system allows the user to invoke a leveling service after placement, enabling the calculation and adjustment of appropriate motor values to restore the desired orientation.

\section{Expressive motion Design}
The framework for generating expressive motion is built on a set of standalone ROS2 animation nodes, each responsible for producing a complete motion sequence that conveys the intended emotion or intent. All nodes combine coordinated body and head motions, eye expressions, and antenna movements using different approaches. Each of the motion sequences can be better explained as animations, as each is hand-crafted using common animation principles. The animations were generated using different approaches, including sinusoidal functions and breaking longer animations into shorter stages and chaining them together. Resulting joint commands were translated to joint commands at runtime using the Inverse kinematics solver. 

All animations share a common design pattern. On startup, each node subscribes to the body pose controller's current Cartesian body pose and uses it as its base pose to start the animation. All subsequent motions are then computed relative to this base pose, allowing animations to be run from any robot configuration and avoiding positional jumps during startup. An additional delay is also applied after the base pose is collected to allow the robot time to settle into its current state.

The final designs for each animation were guided by the animation principles discussed in the thesis's background section. Particular animation principles used include anticipation, slow-in and slow-out, secondary action, and exaggeration. Anticipation was deployed in multiple animations to telegraph upcoming actions; slow-in and slow-out were used to create smooth, natural motion, and a cosine ease interpolation was applied to all stage transitions. Secondary action is achieved in all the animations by coordinating different antenna and eye movements to complement and reinforce larger actions. Exaggeration is also applied by amplifying motion beyond a natural limit to enhance the intended emotional response further.

\subsection{Forward Kinematics}
The forward kinematics deployed in this project describe the translation from joint angles to the position and orientation of the robot's end-effector, in this case, being the robot's foot position relative to the base frame. By following the DH convention introduced in the background chapter, the kinematic chain of the robot's legs consists of five joints: [hip yaw, hip roll, hip pitch, knee pitch, ankle pitch]. The forward kinematics transformation matrix can then be defined by multiplying the individual joint transformation matrices:

\begin{equation}
    T^0_n = A_1 A_2 \cdots A_n
\end{equation}

IN the context of this project, the forward kinematics were primarily used for simplistic state estimation. Given the current joint position of each actuator, the forward kinematics would compute the current foot position relative to the body. This information was used to update the robot state node, allowing the user to monitor the robot's pose during operation and to provide a base pose reference for the animation nodes that calculate motion offsets.
\subsection{Inverse Kinematics}
The inverse kinematics solver computes the joint angles required to place the feet at a desired position, given a commanded body pose. Rather than relying on a numerical iterative solver, an analytical closed-form solution was derived for the final robot. This approach was made possible due to the design choice of using a spherical hip joint, allowing the decoupling of the position and orientation of the leg. This approach was preferred because it could be solved in closed form without relying on more computationally expensive numerical methods, which suited the needs of the final system.

\textbf{coordinate convention and Frame transformations}
Body pose commands are expressed in the robot's base frame and represent a 6-DOF Cartesian pose, with position [x, y, z] in millimeters and orientation [roll, pitch, yaw] in degrees. The body pose controller transforms these commands into per-leg foot-position targets expressed relative to the hip's center, accounting for the robot's lateral and vertical hip offsets and the geometric effects of the body's roll and pitch on the required foot placement. The relative hip-relative foot positions are then passed to the analytical IK solver for each leg independently.

\textbf{Analytical IK Solution}
The IK solver proceeds in two geometric stages. In the initial stage, the roll angle and the effective vertical distance in the leg plane  $y_1$ are computed from the lateral and vertical components of the hip-relative foot position, removing the contribution of the foot offset $d_{shoulder} = 82.0$mm:

\begin{equation}
    y_1 = \sqrt{y_{IK}^2 + z_{IK}^2 - d_{shoulder}^2}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    \theta_{roll} = \arctan2(z_{IK} - d_{shoulder},\ y_{IK})
\end{equation}

In the second stage, the knee and hip pitch angles are solved using a two-link planar system. IK formulation in the sagittal plane. The distance from the hip joint center to the foot is:

\begin{equation}
    d = \sqrt{x_{IK}^2 + y_1^2}
\end{equation}

The knee angle can be obtained by using the cosine rule applied to the triangle formed by the femur, tibia, and the line from the hip to the foot.

\begin{equation}
    \theta_{knee} = \arccos\left(\frac{d^2 - l_1^2 - l_2^2}
    {-2 l_1 l_2}\right) - 180\degree
\end{equation}

where $l_1 = 149.0$mm is the femur length and 
$l_2 = 160.0$mm is the tibia length. The hip pitch angle 
is then:

\begin{equation}
    \theta_{hip} = -\left(\arcsin\frac{l_2 \sin\theta_{knee}}
    {d} - \arctan\frac{x_{IK}}{y_1}\right)
\end{equation}

Finally, the ankle pitch angle is derived to maintain the 
foot parallel to the ground throughout all body poses:

\begin{equation}
    \theta_{ankle} = -(\theta_{hip} + |\theta_{knee}|)
\end{equation}

The hip roll angle is mirrored for the left leg to account
