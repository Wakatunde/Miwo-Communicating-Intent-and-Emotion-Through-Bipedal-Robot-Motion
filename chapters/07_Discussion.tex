\chapter{Discussion}
\section{Expressive Motion and Emotion Recognition}
This chapter aims to discuss and interpret the findings presented in the results chapter while also examining them in the context of the research objectives defined in Chapter 1 of the thesis. 
This section will initially examine the emotion recognition results, looking further into which animations succeeded, which fell short, and what this means for the goal of communicating emotion through biped motion. Furthermore, the Likert and Godspeed results are discussed, with an emphasis on how overall perceptions of the robot inform its suitability for human-robot interaction. Where relevant, differences between video-based and live results are examined, and possible explanations are considered.

\section{Emotion recognition}
The four strongest performing animations include:  \textit{Happiness}, 
\textit{Sadness}, \textit{Anger}, and \textit{Fear}. This is likely not a coincidence, as all these emotions can be classified as basic, universal human emotions within Paul Ekman's framework \cite{ekman1999basic}. It is stated further that these basic emotions are associated with distinctive, cross-culturally recognized physical expressions, which may explain why many participants reliably classified them across both study groups. The work of Beck et al. further supports this suggestion \cite{beck_interpretation_2010}, who demonstrated that happiness, sadness, and fear could be successfully communicated through a humanoid robot's body language in the absence of facial expressions, concluding that the absence of facial expression is not a barrier for emotional communication.

However, \textit{Fear} displays a notable exception to this pattern by achieving a recognition rate of 81\%, the 
video-based study scored considerably lower at only 36\%, representing one of the largest gaps between the two groups. This suggests that, although fear has a well-established physical pattern, the animation may rely on subtler motion cues that could be harder to perceive in video. Another reason for this discrepancy may be how the video demonstration was filmed. The videos offer only a fixed perspective for evaluation, but participants in the live trial had the option to move and observe the robot from different angles, potentially providing more context. This is also in line with the work of \cite{beck_interpretation_2010}, which notes that fear was among the harder emotions to recognize, suggesting that, despite being a basic emotion, its physical expression may be more context-dependent and sensitive to observational conditions.

The other emotion/intent animations, including \textit{Curiosity} and \textit{Frustrated}, are not considered basic emotions in Ekman's framework. They are more complex, socially constructed states, possibly lacking the well-known hardwired physical expressions associated with basic emotions. This could explain why these animations were perceived as more ambiguous across both study groups. 

\textit{Curiosity} was the weakest performing animation overall, achieving consistently low recognition rates in both study groups. Unlike happiness or anger, curiosity is more of a cognitive state and does not offer a strongly defined set of physical expressions. This animation was designed to simulate an intention rather than an emotional state. making it more difficult to communicate through body language alone. The high concentration of \textit{other} responses suggests that participants perceived the motion without being able to map it to a specific emotion or intent. This is also consistent with the findings in \cite{beck_interpretation_2010}, where recognition rates dropped noticeably for emotional states lacking clear physical expression patterns. Improving the curiosity animation may require additional expressive actions, such as clearer use of the eye displays and antenna movement. 

The \textit{Frustrated} animation presented a different challenge as it had a larger concentration of \textit{adjacent} responses rather than being misclassified entirely. Consistent adjacent responses include disgust, impatience, and being upset. This suggests that the animation successfully communicated a negative state even if the intended emotion was not identified. Frustration also shares physical characteristics with anger and disgust, both of which are basic emotions in Ekman's framework \cite{ekman_basic_1999}, which may explain why participants reliably perceived it as a negative state. The large gap between recognition rates in live and video-based trials further suggests that the frustrated animation relies on subtler physical cues that are more easily picked up in a live demonstration.

\section{Live vs Video Study Differences}
The overall trend in the studies' results is that the live-based approach consistently outperformed the video-based approach in recognition rates and Likert-scale results. Based on the study's design, several factors may explain the performance gaps between the two study groups.
The large performance gaps may be a result of the observation methods applied. Observing the robot live, being able to witness the physical attributes like sound, size, and mechanics, could provide participants with additional information, making it easier to interpret the intended emotions or intentions of the robot platform. The video-based participants were presented with a fixed, top-down view of the robot, which may have limited their field of view, depth perception, and ability to observe subtle cues, making the animations less clear. With this in mind, it could be argued that observing the robot physically granted participants an advantage in emotion recognition, causing them to give higher, more accurate scores. 
Another possible reason for the variations is the large differences in the demographics of the two studies. The live study had a participant composition in which 88\% were engineers, compared to 50\% in the video-based study. The Live study had a larger population of participants reporting a high level of robot familiarity. Given that the live study consisted mostly of engineers with greater familiarity with robots, it could be argued that the participants would be more accustomed to robot motions, leading them to rate the robot higher.

The reason for the live-based study consistently outperforming the video-based study is likely a combination of the 2 factors discussed above. The fixed camera perspective may have obscured subtle motion cues that would have been more apparent in person. At the same time, the higher proportion of engineering students in the live group may have made them more attuned to interpreting the mechanical motion.
