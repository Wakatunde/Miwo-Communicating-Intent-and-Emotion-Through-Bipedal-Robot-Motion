
\chapter{Discussion}
This chapter aims to discuss and interpret the findings presented in the results chapter while also examining them in the context of the research objectives defined in Chapter 1 of the thesis. 
This section will initially examine the emotion recognition results, looking further into which animations succeeded, which fell short, and what this means for the goal of communicating emotion through biped motion. Furthermore, the Likert and Godspeed results are discussed, with an emphasis on how overall perceptions of the robot inform its suitability for human-robot interaction. Where relevant, differences between video-based and live results are examined, and possible explanations are considered.

\section{Emotion recognition}
The four strongest performing animations include:  \textit{Happiness}, 
\textit{Sadness}, \textit{Anger}, and \textit{Fear}. This is likely not a coincidence, as all these emotions can be classified as basic, universal human emotions within Paul Ekman's framework \cite{ekman1999basic}. It is stated further that these basic emotions are associated with distinctive, cross-culturally recognized physical expressions, which may explain why many participants reliably classified them across both study groups. The work of Beck et al. further supports this suggestion \cite{beck_interpretation_2010}, who demonstrated that happiness, sadness, and fear could be successfully communicated through a humanoid robot's body language in the absence of facial expressions, concluding that the absence of facial expression is not a barrier for emotional communication.

However, \textit{Fear} displays a notable exception to this pattern by achieving a recognition rate of 81\%, the 
video-based study scored considerably lower at only 36\%, representing one of the largest gaps between the two groups. This suggests that, although fear has a well-established physical pattern, the animation may rely on subtler motion cues that could be harder to perceive in video. Another reason for this discrepancy may be how the video demonstration was filmed. The videos offer only a fixed perspective for evaluation, but participants in the live trial had the option to move and observe the robot from different angles, potentially providing more context. This is also in line with the work of \cite{beck_interpretation_2010}, which notes that fear was among the harder emotions to recognize, suggesting that, despite being a basic emotion, its physical expression may be more context-dependent and sensitive to observational conditions.

The other emotion/intent animations, including \textit{Curiosity} and \textit{Frustrated}, are not considered basic emotions in Ekman's framework.
